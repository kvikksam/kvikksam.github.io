Specimen Conservation Techniques

Corrosion casting is a technique used to study the inside of hollow structures, like blood vessels, by making a solid model of them. A polymeric material, such as silicon rubber, is injected into the blood vessels, creating a detailed mold once it hardens. The surrounding tissue is then removed, leaving behind a clear replica of the vessels. This method allows scientists to study the detailed structure of blood vessels and other hollow organs, giving insights into anatomy and biology.

This process involves several steps to ensure high-quality results:

1. Choosing the Specimen and Access Points

The specimen should be fresh, as older samples may leak. The access point is where the liquid is injected, usually through a major blood vessel leading to the area of interest. Additionally, a more remote access point compared to the area of interest can also be of interest as this will avoid leakage of the casting medium through severed vessels at the level of interest. Furthermore, multiple possible access points can be considered.

Picking a good outflow point, where fluids can exit, is also important, as this ensures the material flows through the entire system properly. The drainage point must be chosen so that injected fluids can only exit from here after they have passed the area of interest.

2. Preparing the access point

A thin-walled tube or catheter that most closely matches the inner diameter of the vessel is chosen as an access point. Subsequently, it is firmly attached to the vessel or duct by suturing it to the structure itself and surrounding tissues to prevent leaks.

3. Flushing the vascular system to be casted

Before injecting the casting mixture, the blood vessels are cleaned with a saline solution. This rinsing step clears out any blood that could block the liquid polymer and tests for leaks.

4. Preparing and injecting the casting mixture

The polymer mix is prepared based on specific instructions such as the manufacturer's instructions, personal experience and recommendations from the literature. The volumes to be prepared are of course tissue-specific, but also depend on the desired filling level and viscosity of the medium. It is important to remove air bubbles from the mix to avoid imperfections. The liquid is then injected into the blood vessels using a syringe.

5. Curing of the resin

The resin should be given sufficient time to harden. Commonly, 12–24 hours is typically given to complete the polymerization. During this time, the position of the specimen can be adjusted in the beginning, but it is better to leave it undisturbed.

6. Maceration or corrosion

In the maceration step, the soft tissues surrounding the cast are removed to fully reveal the cast. Strong chemicals or enzymes may be used for this step. These solutions may also dissolve bones, depending on the process.

7. Rinsing the cast

The cast is rinsed under warm running water to remove leftover tissue. Pulses of high-pressure water can be used to remove loose tissue remnants. However, care should be taken not to break the cast by the force of the water. For the final rinse, clean water like distilled water is used to prevent any contamination.

8. Drying the cast

As a final step before examination, the specimen must be dried and therefore be removed from the fluids it is floating in. Large casts can air-dry, but small or fragile casts may require special techniques like critical point drying or freeze-drying to avoid damage.

9. Examining the cast

Once fully dried, the cast can immediately be examined with the naked eye, under a microscope, or using advanced imaging techniques like CT scans. Mounting the cast on a solid base helps during these examinations.
